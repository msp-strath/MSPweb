Spider Credits

Spider Credits are the faculty's system to record your "personal development" during the time of your PhD degree. To be able to graduate, you will have to record 60 credits, organised into 5 categories, and write a summative essay for each category at the end.

The table with the your credit progress is on the Spider system, under: myStuff → postgraduate records → PG Credits.

For recording credits, you can use activities that you do during your PhD anyway (like attending a conference, or giving a talk), or attend courses which you have to book through the University's system. The official list of all possible activities that count towards credits is here. The list below may be more useful though!

You can add activities to the current academic year. In the end, it doesn't matter in which year you claim your credits (or whether the activity actually happened in the year you claim it). You can also already claim credits which you have not done yet, which might help with planning.

For each activity you claim you have to submit a document with "evidence" of the activity. Don't outdo yourself here, it can just be something like a screenshot of a registration email for a workshop, or a document with a link to a personal blog etc.

Here are some suggestions on which activities to use for the 5 columns:

  • RD901 "Researcher Knowledge and Intellectual Abilities" — 20 credits
    This column requires double the amount of credits than any other one, but it should be fairly easy to fill with:
    • off-campus external training course (any spring or summer school)
    • reading research papers
    • conference attendance
    • workshop attendance
    • internship with external partner (there's a lot of credits in here!)
    • research group meeting attendance (use MSP101, MSP201)
    • English language classes
  • RD902 "Researcher Personal Effectiveness" — 10 credits
    Probably the hardest one to fill with activities you do anyway, so you may have to do some courses:
    • achievement of an award of prize
    • conference volunteer
    • elected member of an external organisation (maybe your hobby comes in handy here?)
    • organising a conference (e.g. SPLS)
    • course "Postgraduate Research Student Induction" (this one you probably do anyway)
    • course "Engaging Beyond Academia: Industry, Networking and Opportunity" (on MyPlace, so you can do it in your own time, and gives quite a lot of credits)
  • RD903 "Research Governance and Organisation" — 10 credits
    # Not the easiest one to fill, so you may have to do some courses:
    • obtaining external/departmental/faculty/etc. travel and training funds (if you've ever applied for funding for going to a conference), you can use this one quite often
    • chairing a session at a conference
    • sitting on a departmental/faculty/university committee (as PGR rep)
    • course "PG Essentials: Digital Scholarship Skills" (on MyPlace, quite a lot of credits)
    • course "Research Integrity (Online)" (on MyPlace)
  • RD904 "Researcher Engagement, Influence and Impact" — 10 credits
    Should be very easy to fill with:
    • oral presentation at peer-reviewed international conference (10 credits!!)
    • oral presentation at local/national conference (e.g. SPLS)
    • poster presentation
    • research group meeting presentation (MSP101!)
    • make a significant contribution to the preparation of a peer reviewed publication
    • teaching/demonstrating/tutoring (~5 credits per course and term, up to 3 times)
  • RD905 "Researcher Professional Development Elective" — 10 credits
    You can use anything that counts towards any other column here. (So probably things from RD901 or RD904...)

There is a few very loosely specified activities, which may be useful if you can argue well enough that you've achieved them:

  • independent development of your ability to work with others, cognitive abilities, communication, creativity, engagement and impact, knowledge base, personal qualities, etc.
  • recognised prior learning
